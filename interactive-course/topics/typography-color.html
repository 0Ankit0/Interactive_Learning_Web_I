Color Harmony

Learn to combine colors effectively using color theory principles. Understanding color relationships helps create balanced, aesthetically pleasing designs that evoke the right emotions and improve user experience.

Complementary

Colors opposite each other on the color wheel. Creates high contrast and vibrant look when used together.

Analogous

Colors next to each other on the wheel. Creates harmonious, comfortable designs with natural feel.

Triadic

Three colors evenly spaced on the wheel. Offers strong visual contrast while retaining balance.

Complementary Colors

Complementary colors create maximum contrast and visual interest. Use one color as dominant and the other for accents. Perfect for call-to-action buttons and highlighting important elements.

Color Harmony Best Practices

  • 60-30-10 Rule: Use 60% dominant color, 30% secondary, and 10% accent color
  • Limit Your Palette: Stick to 3-5 colors maximum to maintain visual coherence
  • Consider Context: Different cultures associate different meanings with colors
  • Test Accessibility: Ensure sufficient contrast for readability and inclusivity
  • Use Neutrals: Gray, white, and black help balance bright colors

Contrast & Accessibility

Ensuring your color choices meet accessibility standards is crucial for creating inclusive web experiences. Proper contrast ratios make content readable for users with visual impairments, color blindness, or viewing content in challenging conditions like bright sunlight.

WCAG Contrast Standards

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) define minimum contrast ratios to ensure text is readable by people with moderately low vision.

AA

Level AA (Minimum)

  • Normal text: 4.5:1 ratio
  • Large text: 3:1 ratio
  • UI components: 3:1 ratio
AAA

Level AAA (Enhanced)

  • Normal text: 7:1 ratio
  • Large text: 4.5:1 ratio
  • Best practice: Highest accessibility

Note: Large text is defined as 18pt (24px) or 14pt (18.66px) bold and larger.

Color Blindness Considerations

Approximately 8% of men and 0.5% of women have some form of color vision deficiency. Design with these principles in mind:

Don't rely on color alone

Use icons, patterns, or text labels alongside colors

Use high contrast

Ensure sufficient brightness difference between colors

Test your design

Use color blindness simulators to verify accessibility

Accessibility Best Practices

  • Start with high contrast: Begin with black on white, then adjust as needed
  • Test with real users: Include people with visual impairments in your testing process
  • Use accessibility tools: Browser extensions and automated checkers catch many issues
  • Consider context: Outdoor viewing requires higher contrast than indoor use
  • Provide alternatives: Offer high-contrast modes or themes for users who need them
  • Focus on content: Ensure links, buttons, and important text have the highest contrast
